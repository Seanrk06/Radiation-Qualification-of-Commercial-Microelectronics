Abstract

Rising investments in the space industry has created greater demand for microelectronics usable in space vehicles. However, designing microelectronic components is challenging, due to the harsh environment, along with stringent weight and size constraints. This work focuses on designing a test protocol for an off-the-shelf component, the FTLX8574D3BCV Multimode 10G SFP+ optical transceiver, to measure its susceptibility to ionizing radiation and charged particles. Preliminary results suggest that combining high atomic-number materials (e.g., tungsten) to shield gamma radiation with complementary layers containing boron for neutron moderation can reduce the total shielding mass, compared to previously published shield designs. These findings offer an alternative solution for designing space-capable transceivers to allow for high-speed data transmission at low bit error rates. By evaluating an off the shelf component for space applications, this research presents a cost-effective option that may help reduce testing costs and accelerate qualification time for new parts, which is greatly needed in the growing space industry today.

Introduction

Shielding a commercial FTLX8574D3BCV 10G SFP+ optical transceiver for space requires balancing both radiation protection and SWaP (size, weight, and power) constraints. In space, the device will be exposed to ionizing radiation from protons (e.g. solar wind, SPEs), heavy ions (cosmic rays), and high-energy photons (gamma rays). Below, we explore lightweight, compact shielding solutions considering mechanical and thermal limitations as well as identify materials (and combinations) that efficiently attenuate these radiation types. We also compare shielding candidates against a baseline of 2 mm aluminum (Al), which is known to be insufficient against high energy protons. We modeled each candidate using TRIM (Transport of Ions in Matter), and have provided the results of such research below.

Mechanical and Thermal Constraints Identified for Shielding Materials

Weight Constraints: The transceiver has a specific cross-section, and thick shielding drastically increases mass. We aim for the minimal required thickness to reduce weight.

Thermal Constraints: The FTLX8574D3BCV may dissipate ~1 W of heat. Overly insulating materials (e.g., some plastics) can cause overheating, so partial metal surfaces or thermally conductive layers can be important.

Structural Considerations: Polymers like pure polyethylene can outgas in space. Often, they require an encapsulating metallic foil for barrier protection and rigidity.

Primary Shielding Strategy (“The Graded-Z Approach”)

This research follows a graded-Z strategy, combining high atomic-number (Z) materials (e.g., tungsten, tantalum) with hydrogenous, low-Z materials (e.g., polyethylene). The high-Z layer absorbs and scatters gamma rays and electrons, while the low-Z layer further moderates and absorbs secondary radiation (like neutrons). NASA has recognized these multi-layer approaches as effective in mitigating secondary particle generation and penetration.

By layering tungsten or tantalum foil between polymer sheets (possibly boron-doped), we reduce overall thickness compared to using a single thick shield of aluminum or steel. This multi-layer method is intended to limit secondary radiation cascades and achieve high overall attenuation per unit mass.

Overall Testing Procedure For All Models

Each candidate design was tested in TRIM (Transport of Ions in Matter) simulations, assuming normal incidence of charged particles (protons, electrons) at various energies (5 MeV, 10 MeV, 50 MeV). The thickness and material composition were varied to evaluate how effectively the stack attenuates or stops the beam. In future hardware tests, we would expose an actual transceiver, plus any chosen shield design, to gamma, proton, and possibly neutron sources, measuring bit error rate (BER) and device functionality at each radiation dose.

This step ensures that real-world usage matches modeling predictions, providing confidence in the part’s reliability for space missions.

Mathematical Justification for Optimal Width

Ideally, the optimal shield thickness is derived by balancing linear attenuation coefficients (for gamma) and nuclear interaction cross-sections (for protons/neutrons). A simplified approach uses the exponential attenuation law (I = I0 e-μx for photons) and stopping power data for charged particles to find the minimal material thickness needed to reduce flux below mission limits.

Conclusion

In conclusion, multi-layer “graded-Z” shielding solutions combining high-Z and low-Z materials show promise in protecting an off-the-shelf 10G optical transceiver from ionizing radiation. Modern references suggest that tungsten, tantalum, or other high-Z foils coupled with hydrogenous layers (HDPE, boron-doped polyethylene, silicone composites) can outperform single-material aluminum shields. The TRIM-based modeling and eventual real-world testing will help finalize a design that balances SWaP constraints, thermal performance, and radiation hardness, with the aim of accelerating qualification of commercial parts for space.
